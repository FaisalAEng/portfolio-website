Spring-Powered “Space” Elevator Prototype

Engineering Design Competition — First Place

Project Summary

The Spring-Powered Elevator Prototype project focused on the design, fabrication, and testing of a purely mechanical system capable of climbing a vertical steel pole using energy stored in a constant-force coil spring. The project was conducted as part of a team-based mechanical engineering design course, with final performance evaluated through a competitive pole-climbing demonstration.

The primary objective was to develop a lightweight and mechanically simple device that could climb a 7 ft (84 in) vertical pole as quickly and reliably as possible while adhering to strict constraints on materials, mass, and type of energy input. No electrical or motorized components were permitted.

The final prototype successfully climbed 62 in (≈81% of the pole height) in approximately 0.6 seconds, achieving first place among all competing teams.

Problem Statement

Background and Motivation

While full-scale space elevators remain theoretical, the engineering challenge of efficiently climbing a fixed vertical structure has practical relevance to inspection robots, maintenance systems, and hazardous-environment automation.

This project used a vertical steel pole as an analogue for a space elevator cable. The challenge was to design a purely mechanical climbing mechanism without motors, electronics, or external power sources.

Project Context and Contributions

The project was completed as a team-based design exercise over a single academic term. All teams were provided with the same limited set of materials and evaluated during a timed competition.

My contributions focused on mechanical concept development, gear-train analysis, CAD modeling in SolidWorks, CAM preparation, CNC machining, and iterative refinement based on testing and performance feedback.

Assumptions and Constraints

Assumptions

  • Uniform surface properties along the steel pole
  • Constant friction between the drive roller and pole
  • Minimal but non-negligible energy losses due to friction
  • Approximately constant force output from the spring

Design Constraints

  • Only supplied materials permitted
  • No motors, electronics, or electrical energy storage
  • System must remain intact throughout the climb
  • Strict mass limitations
  • Single energy release from the spring
  • Limited fabrication time and CNC access

Design Methodology

A structured engineering design process was followed, including requirements definition, concept generation, evaluation, and selection.

Multiple concepts were considered, including inchworm-style mechanisms and braking-based climbers. A wheel-driven approach was selected due to its continuous motion capability, mechanical simplicity, and favorable torque-to-speed characteristics.

Analytical calculations were performed to estimate torque requirements, gear ratios, and expected climb performance. CAD models were used to validate packaging, alignment, and manufacturability prior to fabrication.

Engineering Design and Implementation

The final prototype consisted of a constant-force coil spring, a two-stage spur gear train, a primary drive roller, and a lightweight structural frame.

The gear ratios were selected to balance torque output and rotational speed, ensuring sufficient traction while minimizing energy losses. Structural components were primarily CNC-machined from MDF, with select components fabricated using FDM 3D printing.

Testing and Validation

Bench testing verified gear alignment, spring performance, and roller traction prior to competition. Incremental testing identified frictional losses and structural issues.

During the final competition, the prototype climbed 62 in of the 84 in pole in approximately 0.6 seconds, maintaining stable operation throughout the ascent.

Results and Discussion

The prototype achieved the fastest climb and greatest height among all teams, demonstrating effective conversion of limited spring energy into mechanical work.

Performance limitations were primarily attributed to frictional losses and finite spring energy. Further improvements could be achieved through lighter materials and optimized gear profiles.

Conclusion and Recommendations

This project demonstrated the successful design and fabrication of a high-performance, purely mechanical climbing mechanism under strict material, mass, and time constraints.

Future work could explore alternative materials, improved friction management, and more efficient energy storage mechanisms.
