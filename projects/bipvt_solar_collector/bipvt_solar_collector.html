Project Overview

This capstone project focused on designing and optimizing a building-integrated photovoltaic/thermal (BIPV/T) solar air collector for residential heating. The system recovers heat from PV panels while maintaining lower panel temperatures to improve electricity generation.

Simulations and calculations guided the evaluation of passive, cost-effective design modifications such as airflow baffles and turbulence-enhancing features. The project applied principles of fluid mechanics, heat transfer, and thermal system design within a collaborative, team-based capstone environment.

Multiple airflow enhancement concepts were explored to improve convective heat transfer and outlet air temperature. The final design demonstrates how modest changes to the air channel can efficiently extract thermal energy, benefiting both heating efficiency and PV performance.

Problem Definition & Context

Residential heating in Ontario has historically relied on natural gas due to low fuel costs. Rising energy prices and climate targets are motivating low-carbon heating solutions.

Solar photovoltaic (PV) panels convert only part of the sun’s energy into electricity; the rest is lost as heat, which reduces PV efficiency and can degrade materials over time.

BIPV/T systems recover heat from the underside of PV panels for space heating or as preheated intake for heat pumps. Effective thermal recovery requires well-distributed airflow and turbulence within the air channel.

System Description

The BIPV/T system consists of PV panels mounted above a sealed air channel. Outdoor air flows through the channel, absorbing heat from the panel underside. The heated air can be used for space heating or as preheated intake for a heat pump.

  • Air Channel: Guides airflow beneath PV panels.
  • Fan: Ensures consistent airflow.
  • Enhancement Features: Passive baffles or porous mesh to promote turbulence and mixing.
  • Inlet & Outlet: Designed to improve uniform flow and pressure distribution.

Engineering Objectives & Requirements

  • Maximize convective heat transfer: Extract the most thermal energy from PV panels.
  • Maintain turbulent flow: Ensure effective mixing along the channel.
  • Increase outlet air temperature: Without excessive fan power or airflow speed.
  • Minimize pressure losses: Ensure stable operation.
  • Residential suitability: Simple, low-cost, easy rooftop integration.

Design Concepts Explored

Baseline Configuration

No internal airflow enhancements were used. This served as a reference for evaluating other strategies.

Guiding Wall Concept

Internal walls redirected airflow, reducing stagnation zones and promoting uniform flow.

Fin-Based Enhancement

Longitudinal fins increased surface area and disrupted laminar flow, improving heat transfer.

Porous Mesh Enhancement

A porous metal mesh promoted turbulence and mixing throughout the channel with minimal fan power increase.

Interactive idea: slider toggle to compare baseline vs final design airflow.

Modeling & Analysis Approach

  • Airflow Analysis: Steady-state simulations identified velocity profiles and stagnation zones.
  • Turbulence Evaluation: Reynolds number and turbulence intensity quantified enhancement effects.
  • Thermal Performance: Outlet temperature rise measured to evaluate heat transfer.

Key Calculations & Performance Metrics

  • Reynolds Number (Re): Characterizes airflow regime and turbulence intensity.
  • Temperature Rise (ΔT): Difference between outlet and inlet air temperatures.
  • Energy Balance (Q): Thermal energy recovered: Q = ṁ·cp·ΔT
  • Effective Thermal Transmittance (Ueff): Heat transfer from PV surface to airflow: Ueff = Q / (A·ΔTavg)

Results & Performance Evaluation

  • Outlet air temperature increase: ~8.7°C
  • Sustained turbulent flow along the full duct length
  • Higher Reynolds numbers vs baseline
  • Improved PV electrical efficiency (~0.4% per °C reduction in panel temperature)

Design Tradeoffs, Limitations & Future Work

  • Passive vs Active: Chose passive enhancements (fins, mesh) to reduce energy use and complexity.
  • Turbulence vs Pressure Loss: Optimized geometry for efficient mixing with minimal resistance.
  • Manufacturability & Cost: Simplified for easy fabrication and rooftop integration.
  • Flow Uniformity vs Simplicity: Maintained uniform airflow without adaptive control.
  • Simulation-only; no experimental validation.
  • Steady-state assumptions; transient effects not modeled
  • Idealized geometries and materials
  • Pressure loss assessed qualitatively
  • Experimental validation of airflow and temperature rise
  • Transient simulations with solar variability and refined turbulence models
  • Optimization of fins/mesh for heat transfer vs pressure drop
  • Investigate adaptive airflow control (variable fan speed, dampers)
  • Integration with full residential air-source heat pump systems
