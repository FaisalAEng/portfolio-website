Project Overview

[Insert schematic of BIPV/T system – airflow through under-panel duct] [Insert real-world photo of installation/test site]

This capstone project focused on the design and thermal optimization of a building-integrated photovoltaic/thermal (BIPV/T) solar collector for residential heating. The goal was to recover waste heat from photovoltaic panels while lowering their operating temperature to enhance electrical efficiency.

The system was analyzed using simulation-based airflow and heat transfer models, with an emphasis on passive, cost-effective enhancement strategies suitable for residential deployment. The work was completed as a collaborative, team-based capstone project, applying principles of fluid mechanics, heat transfer, and thermal system design.

The project explored multiple airflow enhancement concepts to improve turbulence and convective heat transfer, ultimately evaluating their effectiveness in increasing outlet air temperature and sustaining turbulent flow within the air channel.

Overall, this project demonstrates how relatively simple design modifications can extract useful thermal energy from PV panels, improving both residential heating efficiency and PV electrical performance.

Problem Definition & Context

Residential space heating in Ontario has historically relied on natural gas systems due to low fuel costs and established infrastructure. Rising energy prices, carbon pricing, and stricter climate targets are increasing both the cost and environmental impact of fossil-fuel-based heating. These trends motivate the development of low-carbon, energy-efficient residential heating solutions.

Solar photovoltaic (PV) systems convert only a fraction of incident solar radiation into electricity, with the remainder lost as heat. Elevated PV panel temperatures reduce electrical output and accelerate material degradation. Waste heat is typically dissipated to the environment and underutilized.

Building-integrated photovoltaic/thermal (BIPV/T) systems enable simultaneous electrical and thermal energy generation. Heat extracted from the underside of PV panels can be used for space heating or as preheated intake air for an air-source heat pump. Effective thermal recovery depends on airflow distribution and turbulence within the air channel.

The core problem addressed in this project was limited convective heat transfer between the PV panel surface and airflow in conventional air-channel designs. Insufficient turbulence and poor mixing restrict recoverable thermal energy. This project evaluated passive, cost-effective airflow enhancement strategies suitable for residential-scale implementation.

System Description

The BIPV/T system consists of photovoltaic roof panels mounted above a sealed air channel. Outdoor air is drawn through the channel by a fan, allowing heat from the underside of the PV panels to transfer to the airflow. The heated air can be used directly for space heating or as preheated intake air for an air-source heat pump.

The air channel geometry, inlet configuration, and internal flow features are critical design parameters that govern airflow distribution, turbulence, and thermal performance. Key components include:

  • Air Channel: A narrow duct beneath the PV panels guiding airflow
  • Fan / Airflow Supply: Provides consistent flow through the channel
  • Enhancement Features: Passive modifications such as fin baffles or porous metal mesh to promote turbulence and mixing
  • Inlet & Outlet Configuration: Influences flow uniformity and pressure distribution

The system is designed to maximize heat extraction without significantly increasing airflow velocity or pressure losses, suitable for residential-scale deployment.

Engineering Objectives & Requirements

The BIPV/T system was designed with the following key objectives:

  • Increase convective heat transfer: Maximize recoverable thermal energy from PV panels
  • Maintain turbulent flow: Promote effective mixing along the air channel
  • Maximize outlet air temperature rise: Without significant increase in airflow velocity or fan power
  • Minimize pressure losses: Ensure stable system operation
  • Ensure residential applicability: Simple, low-cost, and easy to integrate on rooftops

Passive airflow enhancements, such as fin baffles and porous media, were implemented to meet these objectives while avoiding active control systems.

Modeling & Analysis Approach

A computational airflow model was developed to evaluate BIPV/T air channel performance under different design concepts. The model captured internal flow, turbulence generation, and convective heat transfer along the duct length.

Key modeling features included:

  • Airflow Analysis: Steady-state simulations to identify velocity profiles, flow distribution, and stagnation zones.
  • Turbulence Evaluation: Reynolds number and turbulence intensity to quantify the effect of enhancements.
  • Thermal Performance: Temperature rise along the duct to evaluate heat transfer from the PV panel surface.

Simulations were conducted under consistent boundary conditions to enable direct comparison of design concepts. Performance metrics were extracted at selected duct locations to analyze trends along the channel.

Key Calculations & Performance Metrics

The performance of each BIPV/T design was evaluated using engineering calculations derived from airflow and thermal simulations. Metrics provided insight into turbulence generation, heat transfer effectiveness, and overall system performance.

Primary calculations included:

  • Reynolds Number (Re): Calculated at multiple duct locations to quantify turbulence intensity.
    Re = ρ V Dh / μ
  • Temperature Rise (ΔT): Difference between inlet and outlet air temperature, indicating convective heat transfer.
  • Energy Balance (Q = ṁ cp ΔT): Determines thermal energy recovered by the airflow.
  • Effective Thermal Transmittance (Ueff): Represents heat transfer from PV panel surface to air: Ueff = Q / (A ΔTavg)

Results & Performance Evaluation

The final design combined fin baffles with a porous metal mesh distributed throughout the air channel. This configuration achieved measurable improvements in both turbulence and heat transfer compared to baseline and intermediate designs.

Key findings include:

  • Sustained turbulent flow along the full duct length, promoting efficient heat transfer
  • Outlet air temperature increase from ~0 °C (baseline) to 8.7 °C
  • Significantly higher Reynolds numbers than baseline and intermediate designs
  • Enhanced heat extraction without increasing airflow velocity
  • Improved PV electrical efficiency (≈0.4% efficiency loss per °C panel temperature rise)

Design Tradeoffs & Decisions

Several tradeoffs were considered to balance thermal performance, system complexity, manufacturability, and residential applicability.

Key tradeoff considerations included:

  • Passive vs. Active Enhancement: Passive features (fins, porous mesh) were favored over increased fan power to reduce energy consumption and complexity.
  • Turbulence vs. Pressure Loss: Porous media improved turbulence but introduced pressure drop; fin geometry and placement optimized turbulence with minimal pressure penalty.
  • Manufacturability & Cost: Enhancement geometries were simplified for ease of fabrication and standard PV panel integration.
  • Flow Uniformity vs. System Simplicity: Design maintained uniform airflow distribution without adaptive control or additional instrumentation.

Limitations & Future Improvements

Limitations of the current study include:

  • Simulation-only analysis; no experimental validation was performed.
  • Steady-state assumptions; transient effects such as varying solar irradiance and ambient temperature were not included.
  • Simplified material and geometry models; fins and porous media were idealized.
  • Pressure loss assessment was qualitative, without detailed CFD or fan system integration.

Future work could address these limitations:

  • Experimental validation of airflow, turbulence, and temperature rise trends.
  • Enhanced modeling incorporating transient solar conditions, refined turbulence models, and detailed material properties.
  • Optimization of enhancement geometry (fins, mesh) for improved heat transfer and minimized pressure loss.
  • Investigation of adaptive airflow control strategies (variable fan speed, automated dampers).
  • Integration with full residential air-source heat pump systems.
