Project Overview

This capstone project focused on designing and optimizing a building-integrated photovoltaic/thermal (BIPV/T) solar air collector for residential heating. The aim was to recover heat from PV panels while keeping their temperature lower to improve electricity generation.

Simulations and calculations guided the evaluation of passive, cost-effective design modifications such as airflow baffles and turbulence-enhancing features. The project applied principles of fluid mechanics, heat transfer, and thermal system design within a collaborative, team-based capstone environment.

Multiple airflow enhancement concepts were explored to improve convective heat transfer and outlet air temperature. The final design demonstrates how modest changes to the air channel can efficiently extract thermal energy, benefiting both heating efficiency and PV performance.

Problem Definition & Context

Residential heating in Ontario has historically relied on natural gas due to low fuel costs. Rising energy prices and climate targets are motivating low-carbon heating solutions.

Solar photovoltaic (PV) panels convert only part of the sun’s energy into electricity; the rest is lost as heat. Excess heat reduces PV efficiency and can degrade materials over time.

BIPV/T systems recover heat from the underside of PV panels for space heating or as preheated intake for heat pumps. Effective thermal recovery requires well-distributed airflow and turbulence within the air channel.

Conventional air channels often have poor mixing and low turbulence, limiting heat recovery. This project addresses these challenges with passive, cost-effective airflow enhancements.

System Description

The BIPV/T system consists of PV panels mounted above a sealed air channel. Outdoor air flows through the channel, absorbing heat from the panel underside. The heated air can be used directly for space heating or as preheated intake for a heat pump.

Key components:

  • Air Channel: Guides airflow beneath PV panels.
  • Fan: Ensures consistent airflow.
  • Enhancement Features: Passive baffles or porous mesh to promote turbulence and mixing.
  • Inlet & Outlet: Designed to improve uniform flow and pressure distribution.

The system balances heat extraction efficiency with low airflow resistance, suitable for residential applications.

Engineering Objectives & Requirements

The BIPV/T system was designed with the following key objectives:

  • Maximize convective heat transfer: Extract the most thermal energy from PV panels.
  • Maintain turbulent flow: Ensure effective mixing along the channel.
  • Increase outlet air temperature: Without excessive fan power or airflow speed.
  • Minimize pressure losses: Ensure stable operation.
  • Residential suitability: Simple, low-cost, easy rooftop integration.

Passive enhancements such as fins and porous mesh were implemented to meet these objectives.

Design Concepts Explored

Several airflow enhancement concepts were developed and evaluated to improve thermal performance:

  • Baseline: No internal enhancements; serves as reference.
  • Guiding Wall: Redirects airflow for uniform distribution.
  • Fin-Based: Longitudinal fins disrupt laminar flow.
  • Porous Mesh: Promotes turbulence with minimal pressure drop.

Simulations compared Reynolds number, velocity, and temperature rise across concepts. The final design combined fin baffles with a porous mesh.

Modeling & Analysis Approach

  • Airflow Analysis: Steady-state simulations identified velocity profiles and stagnation zones.
  • Turbulence Evaluation: Reynolds number and turbulence intensity quantified the effect of enhancements.
  • Thermal Performance: Outlet temperature rise measured to evaluate heat transfer.

Simulations used consistent boundary conditions for fair comparison. Metrics were extracted at key locations to observe trends along the channel.

Key Calculations & Performance Metrics

The performance of each BIPV/T design was evaluated using engineering calculations derived from airflow and thermal simulations. Metrics provided insight into turbulence generation, heat transfer effectiveness, and overall system performance.

Primary calculations included:

  • Reynolds Number (Re): Calculated at multiple duct locations to quantify turbulence intensity.
    Re = ρ V Dh / μ
  • Temperature Rise (ΔT): Difference between inlet and outlet air temperature, indicating convective heat transfer.
  • Energy Balance (Q = ṁ cp ΔT): Determines thermal energy recovered by the airflow.
  • Effective Thermal Transmittance (Ueff): Represents heat transfer from PV panel surface to air: Ueff = Q / (A ΔTavg)

Results & Performance Evaluation

The final design (fin baffles + porous mesh) improved turbulence and heat transfer without increasing airflow velocity.

  • Outlet air temperature increase: ~8.7°C.
  • Sustained turbulent flow along full duct length.
  • Higher Reynolds numbers vs baseline.
  • Improved PV electrical efficiency (~0.4% per °C reduction in panel temperature).

Design Tradeoffs, Limitations & Future Work

Key Tradeoffs:

  • Passive vs Active: Chose passive enhancements (fins, porous mesh) to reduce energy use and complexity.
  • Turbulence vs Pressure Loss: Optimized fin/mesh geometry for efficient mixing with minimal resistance.
  • Manufacturability & Cost: Simplified designs for easy fabrication and rooftop integration.
  • Flow Uniformity vs Simplicity: Maintained uniform airflow without adaptive control.

Limitations:

  • Simulation-only; no experimental validation.
  • Steady-state assumptions; transient effects (sun, ambient) not modeled.
  • Idealized geometries and materials for fins/mesh.
  • Pressure loss assessed qualitatively, no fan system integration.

Future Work:

  • Experimental validation of airflow, turbulence, and temperature rise.
  • Transient simulations with solar variability and refined turbulence models.
  • Optimization of fins/mesh for heat transfer vs pressure drop.
  • Investigate adaptive airflow control (variable fan speed, dampers).
  • Integration with full residential air-source heat pump systems.
