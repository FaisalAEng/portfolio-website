Project Overview

[Hero Image Placeholder]

This capstone project focused on designing and optimizing a building-integrated photovoltaic/thermal (BIPV/T) solar air collector for residential heating. The aim was to recover heat from PV panels while keeping their temperature lower to improve electricity generation.

Simulations and calculations guided the evaluation of passive, cost-effective design modifications such as airflow baffles and turbulence-enhancing features. The project applied principles of fluid mechanics, heat transfer, and thermal system design within a collaborative, team-based capstone environment.

Problem Definition & Context

Residential heating in Ontario has historically relied on natural gas. Rising energy prices and climate targets are motivating low-carbon heating solutions.

Solar photovoltaic (PV) panels convert only part of the sun’s energy into electricity; the rest is lost as heat. Excess heat reduces PV efficiency and can degrade materials over time.

System Description

The BIPV/T system consists of PV panels mounted above a sealed air channel. Heated air can be used directly for space heating or as preheated intake for a heat pump.

  • Air Channel: Guides airflow beneath PV panels.
  • Fan: Ensures consistent airflow.
  • Enhancement Features: Passive baffles or porous mesh.
  • Inlet & Outlet: Designed to improve uniform flow.

Engineering Objectives & Requirements

Key objectives of the system:

  • Maximize convective heat transfer
  • Maintain turbulent flow
  • Increase outlet air temperature
  • Minimize pressure losses
  • Residential suitability

Results & Performance Evaluation

[Results Graph Placeholder]

  • Outlet air temperature increase: ~8.7°C
  • Sustained turbulent flow along duct
  • Higher Reynolds numbers vs baseline
  • Improved PV electrical efficiency (~0.4% per °C reduction)

Design Tradeoffs & Limitations

  • Passive vs Active enhancements
  • Turbulence vs Pressure Loss
  • Manufacturability & Cost
  • Flow Uniformity vs Simplicity
  • Simulation-only; no experimental validation
  • Steady-state assumptions; transient effects not modeled
