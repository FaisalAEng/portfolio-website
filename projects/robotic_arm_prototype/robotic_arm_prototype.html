Simple Robotic Arm Prototype

Servo-Driven Multi-Link Manipulator

Project Summary

This project involved the design and construction of a low-cost robotic arm prototype to explore fundamental robotic system design, servo-based actuation, and Arduino control. Built using inexpensive materials and minimal tooling, the prototype was intended as a proof-of-concept learning platform rather than a high-performance manipulator.

The robotic arm successfully demonstrated basic pick-and-place functionality for lightweight objects, providing hands-on insight into mechanical layout, control logic, and practical limitations of rapid prototyping with low-cost materials.

Problem Statement & Objectives

Problem Statement

Design a simple robotic arm capable of picking up a lightweight object, repositioning it through horizontal and vertical motion, and releasing it at another location, using minimal materials and an Arduino-based control system.

Project Objectives

  • Build a functional robotic arm using low-cost, readily available materials
  • Implement multi-joint servo control using an Arduino UNO
  • Validate mechanical and control concepts through physical testing
  • Identify design limitations and improvement opportunities for future iterations

System Overview

Mechanical Architecture

  • Total arm length: ~20 cm
  • Link lengths: ~10 cm per segment
  • Degrees of Freedom:
    • Base rotation (horizontal motion)
    • Arm rotation (vertical motion)
    • Servo-driven gripper (open/close)

The arm was constructed using wooden sticks, cardboard, string, and craft glue. All joints were servo-actuated revolute joints. The base was not externally mounted, resulting in instability under load and requiring manual stabilization during operation.

End Effector (Gripper)

  • One servo-driven movable claw
  • One stationary claw
  • Rubber band friction surfaces to improve grip

This configuration reduced complexity and improved reliability compared to an earlier dual-motor gripper concept.

Electronics and Control

Actuation and Hardware

  • Controller: Arduino UNO R3
  • Motors: SG90 9g servo motors
  • Power: Arduino 5 V rail

No power brownout or overheating issues were observed during testing; configuration is suitable only for low-load operation.

Control Strategy

  • Open-loop servo position control (no feedback sensors)
  • Pushbuttons for continuous joint rotation
  • Slide switch for gripper open/close control
  • Servo angle limits tuned to prevent over-rotation (gripper: 50° open, 120° closed)

Simulation

Circuit and control logic were first developed and tested in TinkerCAD, allowing verification of motor behavior and input logic before physical assembly.

Design Iterations

Initial Concept

  • Four servo motors: two for arm motion, two for gripper claws
  • Button-based control for all joints
  • Increased wiring complexity and limited breadboard space

Refined Design

  • Reduced to three servo motors
  • Simplified gripper with a single actuated claw
  • Replaced gripper buttons with a slide switch to conserve I/O space
  • Removed unnecessary linkages to improve stability and weight distribution

These changes improved usability and robustness without sacrificing required functionality.

Testing and Results

Degrees of Freedom and Motion

  • DoF 1: Base rotation (horizontal motion)
  • DoF 2: Arm rotation (vertical motion)
  • Gripper: Opening and closing motion (excluded from DoF count)

Iteration 1 – Initial Concept

Test Scenario: Pick up and relocate a crumpled paper object

Observed Performance: Horizontal and vertical motions were repeatable; gripper successfully grasped and released lightweight objects. Motion smoothness limited by structural flex and servo mounting rigidity.

Limitations Identified: Fragile construction, base instability, limited dexterity. Prototype still completed the pick-and-place task consistently.

Key Engineering Takeaways

  • Mechanical rigidity is critical even for low-load prototypes
  • Simplifying actuation and control improves reliability more than adding complexity
  • Simulation tools are effective for validating control logic before hardware assembly
  • Low-cost materials are sufficient for validating system-level concepts

Future Improvements

  • Replace structural elements with 3D-printed components
  • Use servo mounting brackets and fasteners for improved rigidity
  • Implement potentiometer or joystick-based proportional control
  • Add additional degrees of freedom, such as a wrist joint
  • Redesign the gripper with interchangeable or compliant tips

Conclusion

This robotic arm prototype met its objective as a learning-focused proof-of-concept system. While structurally limited, it successfully demonstrated multi-joint servo control, basic manipulation, and iterative design decision-making. The insights gained inform future designs with improved materials, refined control strategies, and enhanced mechanical performance.
